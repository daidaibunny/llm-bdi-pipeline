\section{Related Work and Positioning}
\label{sec:related-work}

MOOSE learns first-order condition-to-action rules by goal regression from
training plans \cite{Chen2026Moose}. Its synthesis algorithm decomposes
training problems into singleton goal conditions, which makes it the closest
match for our atomic-template layer. The same theory also explains why MOOSE is
not the whole solution for this project: strongly interacting multi-literal
goals, such as Blocksworld tower construction, should be represented through an
explicit temporal or structural controller rather than by assuming arbitrary
goal independence.

Generalized-policy learning with description-logic features studies policies
that solve many instances of a domain using lifted symbolic features
\cite{Bonet2018Features,Frances2021LearningGeneralPolicies,
Bonet2024GeneralPoliciesWidth}. D2L and the KR 2025 learner-policies-from-
examples line are important fallback backends for structural domains, especially
when MOOSE-style singleton regression is not the right artifact. In this paper,
such policies are useful only after their features can be bound to declared
\pddl{} predicates, primitive actions, or predicate achievement goals.

Policy sketches express domain-general subgoal structure as qualitative feature
rules \cite{Drexler2024SubgoalSketches}, and learner-sketches learns such rules
automatically \cite{Drexler2022LearningSketches}. Sketches remain useful
comparison artifacts and possible backend outputs, but a raw sketch is not yet
an \asl{} plan library. It normally guides a planner that fills in subproblems;
our compiler must instead produce executable predicate-goal plans.

Lifted decision-list policies and planning-program synthesis provide additional
generalized-planning baselines. PG3 searches over lifted decision-list policies
\cite{Yang2022PG3}, while BFGP++ searches over terminating programs with loops
\cite{SegoviaAguas2022BFGPpp}. These representations can be powerful, but they
do not directly target maintained AgentSpeak(L) libraries with atomic predicate
modules and query-specific temporal wrappers.

Temporal extended goals are commonly represented with temporal logics or
automata. We use LTLf and deterministic finite automata as an upper controller
over the achievement-goal library. This separates the reusable domain-level
atomic library from the query-specific temporal objective.
