\begin{abstract}
Generalized planning learns reusable behavior for problem families, whereas
Belief--Desire--Intention (BDI) agents execute plan libraries. Direct adaptation
leaves a representation gap: singleton-goal evidence may omit modules for
subsidiary achievements and does not certify temporal composition. We introduce
\methodname{}, short for Generalized Planning to Plan Libraries. Its typed input
contract maps controlled-language requests to externally bound parametric
\ltlf{} specifications consistent with the planning domain, while its compiler
turns generalized-planning evidence and PDDL action models into certified BDI
plan libraries. GP2PL validates and lifts evidence, generates
schema-derived modules for producible goals, selects a compact closed
atomic core, and appends query controllers guided by deterministic finite
automata for finite-trace temporal goals without relearning that core. For the
generated candidate language and supported fragment, accepted branches and
controllers satisfy conditional soundness guarantees; uncertified obligations
are rejected. Across five fixed evidence seeds, mean held-out coverage over
\BenchmarkDomainCount{} domains is \AtomicFiveSeedMeanSuccessPercent{}\%, with a
sample standard deviation of \AtomicFiveSeedSampleSDPercent{} percentage points.
Recursive candidates improve paired atomic success by 10.42 percentage points,
and preservation certification improves paired temporal success by 9.36
percentage points. All \TranslationEquivalentCount{} controlled-language
translations match their gold finite-trace languages, and all
\TEGProblemCount{} admitted bound queries produce independently validated
accepting traces.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Classical PDDL planning computes an action sequence for one grounded problem
\cite{McDermott1998PDDL}; generalized planning amortizes this effort by learning
reusable behavior for a family of related problems
\cite{Srivastava2011DirectedSearch,Chen2026Moose}. Belief--Desire--Intention
(BDI) agents also reuse behavior, but through plan libraries that connect events
and belief contexts to actions and subsidiary goals
\cite{Rao1996AgentSpeak,MeneguzziDeSilva2015PlanningBDI}. These artifacts obey
different contracts: singleton-goal policy evidence need not provide the
callable, context-indexed modules required by a maintained BDI library.

Consider a planning domain $D$, training instances
$\mathcal I_{\mathrm{train}}$, and raw singleton-goal policy evidence
$\mathcal E_{\mathrm{raw}}$ produced by an external generalized-planning backend.
Such evidence records how individual achievements can be reached under
particular conditions, but need not supply every subsidiary module or the
composition guarantees required by an executable BDI library.

GP2PL addresses this representation gap as shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}. It compiles the evidence once into a certified,
reusable atomic module core, then reuses that core when compiling each bound
temporally extended goal (TEG) \cite{Hindriks2009TemporalGoals} into the same
maintained library. The external backend is neither replaced nor rerun.

A TEG constrains a finite state trace rather than only a final state
\cite{DeGiacomoVardi2013,DeGiacomoRubin2018AutomataFOND}. Conditions required
along that trace may interfere, so an arbitrary sequence of achievement calls
need not preserve the intended temporal behavior
\cite{Thangarajah2003GoalInterference}.

GP2PL makes four contributions: a typed controlled-language contract and
benchmark for bound TEGs with exact semantic translation evaluation; an
evidence-to-library compiler that supplies omitted producible subgoals and
selects a compact, internally closed atomic core; certificate conditions for
executable, terminating, and composable modules; and query-specific temporal
controllers that reuse and extend one maintained BDI library.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

GP2PL translates typed controlled-language requests and compiles GP evidence and
PDDL schemas into a closed atomic core with certified DFA controllers. Across five
seeds, atomic coverage over
\BenchmarkDomainCount{} domains is \AtomicFiveSeedMeanSuccessPercent{}\%
(\AtomicFiveSeedSampleSDPercent{}-point sample SD); recursion and preservation
add 10.42 and 9.36 paired-success points. All
\TranslationEquivalentCount{} translations and \TEGProblemCount{} admitted
queries pass DFA-language and execution validation. Unsupported are unrestricted
PDDL--\ltlf{} synthesis, out-of-fragment arithmetic/formulae, and uncertified
repair. Future work studies cross-seed temporal robustness, candidate-size
scaling, action-quality overhead, same-scope trace-search comparisons, and
richer models under fail-closed certification. Pinned PDDL generators with
held-out-disjoint instances can extend domain onboarding without changing the
certificates.
