\begin{abstract}
Generalized planning seeks programs that solve families of related planning
problems, but a learned policy is not necessarily a plan library that a
Belief--Desire--Intention (BDI) agent can execute. A direct transcription may
leave variables unbound, omit auxiliary achievement modules, introduce
nonterminating recursion, or allow one temporal subgoal to undo another. We
introduce \methodname{} (Generalized Planning to Plan Libraries), which treats
this representation gap as a certified compilation problem. Given singleton-goal
evidence from MOOSE and PDDL action schemas, \methodname{} constructs a closed,
lifted BDI plan library and uses Clingo to choose
a compact set of modules whose binding, executability, completion, progress,
and resource-restoration obligations can be established. We realize the
library in \asl{} and execute it with Jason. For each supported \ltlf{} query,
a separate compiler obtains a deterministic finite automaton from the real
\texttt{ltlf2dfa}/MONA toolchain and generates query-local controllers that
preserve transition guards while a monitor observes every primitive action.
The guarantees are relative to the generated candidate language and declared
temporal fragment; a strategy is rejected when its obligations cannot be
proved. Across \BenchmarkDomainCount{} domains, all
\TranslationEquivalentCount{} model-produced temporal specifications are
language-equivalent to their gold formulas. The resulting
\TEGProblemCount{} bound queries all execute successfully in Jason, and their
traces pass VAL and are accepted by both finite-trace automata.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

A classical planner returns an action sequence for one grounded problem,
whereas a generalized planner seeks behavior reusable across a family of
problems. A Belief--Desire--Intention (BDI) interpreter requires a different
artifact: a plan library connecting events and belief contexts to actions and
subsidiary goals. Converting a generalized policy into such a library is not a
syntactic exercise. Variables must be bound, internal calls implemented,
recursion well founded, and evidence-supported behavior retained.

For example, evidence for \texttt{on(X,Y)} may prescribe
\texttt{stack(X,Y)} when \texttt{holding(X)} and \texttt{clear(Y)} hold. A
reusable module must also handle an obstructed \texttt{Y}, perhaps by invoking
\texttt{!clear(Y)} before retrying \texttt{!on(X,Y)}. Yet the evidence may omit
\texttt{clear} because it never appeared as a training goal. PDDL schemas must
then supply the missing module and a progress argument for its recursion.

Temporally extended goals add a distinct problem. The same domain module should
serve many queries, while
$F(\mathit{on}(X,Y)\land X(F(\mathit{clear}(Z))))$ imposes a query-specific
order. Literals on one automaton transition must also hold in the same observed
state, so a later module may not undo an earlier achievement. Temporal control
must therefore be composed over, rather than learned into, the domain library.

Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} presents \methodname{}. Domain compilation joins
singleton-goal evidence with PDDL schemas to construct one lifted atomic-module
library; query compilation turns supported deterministic finite automaton (DFA)
transitions into controllers over that library. MOOSE supplies the present
evidence, while \asl{} and Jason realize the BDI plan-library model. All
certificates depend on rule structure and symbolic PDDL effects, never on a
domain or predicate name.

GP2PL makes three contributions. It first casts evidence-to-library conversion
as constrained compilation with minimum-cost selection inside an explicit
candidate language. It then introduces symbol-invariant certificates for
binding, symbolic execution, achievement, recursive progress, module closure,
and resource restoration. Finally, it compiles real \ltlf-to-DFA transitions
into declarative controllers whose completion effects preserve signed guard
obligations under primitive-step monitoring. The guarantees are intentionally
relative to the candidate language and temporal fragment: an unproved module or
transition is rejected. Across \BenchmarkDomainCount{} domains, all
\TEGProblemCount{} evaluated queries execute in Jason, pass VAL, and are
accepted by both gold and predicted DFAs; this is coverage of the evaluated
inputs, not completeness for arbitrary PDDL--\ltlf{} products.

\section{Limitations and Scope}
\label{sec:limitations}

MOOSE is the only evaluated evidence provider. Schema-derived sequences are
limited to finite acyclic STRIPS regression; cyclic dependencies require
provider evidence or certified recursion. Resource restoration follows finite,
non-repeating keyed modes; ambiguous roles or incompatible untyped producers
are rejected. Long macros are not assumed equivalent to shorter recursion.

Query guards admit conjunction and literal negation. Negative literals express
absence, not negative achievement calls; mixed Boolean--numeric guards require
complete Boolean effects and constant integer changes. The monitor observes
every primitive state but does not supply a complete strategy. The evaluated
formulas use $F$, $X$, $U$, conjunction, and literal negation, not unrestricted
\ltlf{}.

\section{Conclusion}

GP2PL compiles generalized-planning evidence into BDI plan libraries and
composes temporal controllers over them, realized here in \asl{}/Jason. All
\TEGProblemCount{} queries in \BenchmarkDomainCount{} domains pass Jason,
neutral-goal VAL, and both DFA checks; broader providers and cyclic or
resource-ambiguous strategies remain open.
