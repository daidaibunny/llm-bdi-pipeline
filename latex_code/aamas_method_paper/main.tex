\begin{abstract}
Generalized planning seeks behavior that transfers across related planning
instances, but its policies are not automatically executable plan libraries for
Belief--Desire--Intention (BDI) agents. Direct transcription leaves open whether
variables are bound, subsidiary goals are implemented, recursion progresses,
resources are restored, and temporally related achievements remain compatible.
We formulate \methodname{} (Generalized Planning to Plan Libraries), a
certificate-carrying compilation framework for this representation gap. At the
domain level, \methodname{} combines singleton-goal policy evidence with PDDL
action schemas and selects a compact, closed set of lifted BDI modules subject
to binding, executability, completion, progress, and resource-restoration
conditions. At the query level, it derives controllers from real
\texttt{ltlf2dfa}/MONA automata and admits a transition strategy only when its
signed guard can be established without invalidating protected obligations.
Theorems establish local module soundness, candidate-space optimality,
transition-composition soundness, and trace-faithful monitoring for the declared
fragment; unproved cases are rejected. We instantiate the framework with MOOSE
evidence, Clingo selection, and an \asl{}/Jason realization. Across
\BenchmarkDomainCount{} domains, all \TranslationEquivalentCount{}
model-produced specifications are language-equivalent to their gold formulas,
and all \TEGProblemCount{} bound queries produce traces accepted by Jason, VAL,
and both finite-trace automata.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

A classical planner returns an action sequence for one grounded problem,
whereas a generalized planner seeks behavior reusable across a family of
problems. A Belief--Desire--Intention (BDI) interpreter requires a different
artifact: a plan library connecting events and belief contexts to actions and
subsidiary goals. Converting a generalized policy into such a library is not a
syntactic exercise. Variables must be bound, internal calls implemented,
recursion well founded, and evidence-supported behavior retained.

For example, evidence for \texttt{on(X,Y)} may prescribe
\texttt{stack(X,Y)} when \texttt{holding(X)} and \texttt{clear(Y)} hold. A
reusable module must also handle an obstructed \texttt{Y}, perhaps by invoking
\texttt{!clear(Y)} before retrying \texttt{!on(X,Y)}. Yet the evidence may omit
\texttt{clear} because it never appeared as a training goal. PDDL schemas must
then supply the missing module and a progress argument for its recursion.

Temporally extended goals add a distinct problem. The same domain module should
serve many queries, while
$F(\mathit{on}(X,Y)\land X(F(\mathit{clear}(Z))))$ imposes a query-specific
order. Literals on one automaton transition must also hold in the same observed
state, so a later module may not undo an earlier achievement. Temporal control
must therefore be composed over, rather than learned into, the domain library.

Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} presents \methodname{}. Domain compilation joins
singleton-goal evidence with PDDL schemas to construct one lifted atomic-module
library; query compilation turns supported deterministic finite automaton (DFA)
transitions into controllers over that library. MOOSE supplies the present
evidence, while \asl{} and Jason realize the BDI plan-library model. All
certificates depend on rule structure and symbolic PDDL effects, never on a
domain or predicate name.

GP2PL makes three contributions. It first defines evidence-to-library conversion
as constrained compilation with minimum-cost selection inside an explicit
candidate language. It then introduces symbol-invariant proof obligations for
binding, symbolic execution, achievement, recursive progress, module closure,
and resource restoration. Finally, it defines a preservation relation for real
\ltlf-to-DFA transition guards and realizes the certified order as a
declarative controller under primitive-step monitoring. These guarantees are
relative to the candidate language and temporal fragment: an unproved module or
transition is rejected. Across \BenchmarkDomainCount{} domains, all
\TEGProblemCount{} evaluated queries execute in Jason, pass VAL, and are
accepted by both gold and predicted DFAs; this is coverage of the evaluated
inputs, not completeness for arbitrary PDDL--\ltlf{} products. Complete proofs
and the reproducibility specification appear in the supplementary appendix.

\section{Conclusion}

GP2PL gives certificate conditions for compiling generalized-planning evidence
into reusable BDI plan libraries and composing finite-trace DFA progress over
them. Its \asl{}/Jason realization passes Jason, neutral-goal VAL, and both DFA
checks on all \TEGProblemCount{} queries in \BenchmarkDomainCount{} domains.
The guarantees are limited to the certified candidate language and declared
\ltlf{} fragment; unproved strategies are rejected rather than assumed sound.
