\begin{abstract}
Generalized planners synthesize reusable policies, whereas a
Belief--Desire--Intention interpreter requires an executable, closed plan
library. Direct translation does not establish safe binding, recursive
progress, resource restoration, or preservation among interacting goals. We
present a certificate-carrying compiler that combines MOOSE singleton-goal
evidence with PDDL-schema candidates and uses Clingo to select lifted
AgentSpeak modules. A separate query stage translates parametric temporally
extended goals through the real \ltlf-to-DFA toolchain and emits query-local
balanced repair controllers only when completion effects certify transition
preservation. A primitive-step DFA monitor retains finite-trace semantics, and
uncertified choices are rejected rather than patched. The pinned 16-domain
artifact contains \AtomicSelectedBranchCount{} selected branches from
\AtomicCandidateCount{} certified candidates. GPT-5.5 produced
\TranslationEquivalentCount{}/\TranslationInputCount{} contract-valid
translations exactly language-equivalent to gold; all
\TEGEndToEndSuccessCount{}/\TEGProblemCount{} expanded controllers then
succeeded in Jason, passed neutral-goal VAL, and were accepted by both finite-
trace automata.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

A classical planner returns a plan for one grounded problem. A generalized
planner instead seeks a policy or program reusable across a family of problems.
A practical Belief--Desire--Intention (BDI) agent needs a third artifact: a
maintained plan library with lifted event triggers, belief contexts, primitive
actions, and calls between achievement modules. Converting a generalized
policy into such a library is not a formatting step. Variables must remain
safely bound, called modules must be present, recursive branches must terminate,
and an apparently shorter branch must not silently lose behavior evidenced by
the source policy.

Consider a generalized-planning rule for the singleton goal
\texttt{on(X,Y)}. A finite evidence macro may apply \texttt{stack(X,Y)} when
\texttt{holding(X)} and \texttt{clear(Y)} hold, but a reusable BDI module must
also handle states in which \texttt{Y} is obstructed. The desired lifted
structure can call an internal \texttt{!clear(Y)} module and retry
\texttt{!on(X,Y)}. The helper may be absent from the evidence because
\texttt{clear(X)} was never a training goal; it must instead enter through
PDDL add-effect and precondition closure. This example exposes the central
representation gap: evidence for grounded executions does not by itself define
a closed, terminating AgentSpeak library.

Temporally extended goals (TEGs) introduce a separate difficulty: their truth
depends on a finite state trace rather than only its final state. A domain-level
module such as \texttt{+!on(X,Y)} should remain reusable, while a query such as
$F(\mathit{on}(X,Y)\land X(F(\mathit{clear}(Z))))$ requires query-specific
control. Treating a conjunction on one automaton transition as an unchecked
sequence of achievement calls is unsound when a later call can delete an
earlier result. Conversely, embedding each query into the static domain
library would conflate reusable behavior with temporal intent.

We address both gaps with the two-stage architecture in
Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}. The first stage turns singleton-goal
generalized-planning evidence and PDDL schemas into one lifted atomic-module
library. The second compiles supported deterministic-finite-automaton (DFA)
transition guards into query-local controllers over that library. MOOSE
supplies the evidence in our implementation, but its rules are evidence rather
than finished AgentSpeak code. After normalization, acceptance depends on rule
structure, PDDL schemas, and compiler certificates, never on domain or
predicate names.

The implemented contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
\item a certificate-carrying plan-library compiler that jointly selects
validated singleton-goal evidence and PDDL-schema modules in one Clingo
optimization;
\item domain-independent obligations for lifted binding, local execution,
closure, recursive relational progress, one-step resource release, and staged
preparation; and
\item a real \ltlf-to-DFA query compiler that uses conditional completion
effects to preserve positive and negative transition obligations, emitting
balanced declarative BDI controllers whose query-local DFA monitor advances
after every primitive PDDL action.
\end{itemize}

The claim is intentionally narrower than universal generalized planning or full
temporal synthesis. We prove properties only relative to the generated
candidate space and the supported DFA fragment, and we report rejection when a
required certificate cannot be constructed.

On the clean pinned execution, all \TEGProblemCount{} bindings over
\BenchmarkDomainCount{} domains pass Jason, VAL, and both DFA oracles. The
median trace has \TEGMedianActionCount{} actions and median per-case runtime is
\TEGMedianRuntimeSeconds{} seconds. These numbers characterize the released
bindings and exact hashed libraries, not arbitrary PDDL--\ltlf realizability.

\section{Limitations and Scope}
\label{sec:limitations}

The implemented evidence provider is MOOSE; provider neutrality is an interface
property, not an empirical claim about other backends. Schema-derived action
sequences use finite acyclic STRIPS regression without an arbitrary action-depth
bound. Resource cleanup may traverse a finite non-repeating keyed mode path.
Ambiguous resource orientation and untyped overloaded producers that require
different nested preservation portfolios are rejected. The compiler does not
claim semantic equivalence between an arbitrary long evidence macro and a
short recursive program.

At the query layer, transition guards may use conjunction and literal negation.
Negative literals are signed obligations, not negative achievement calls; a
positive sibling branch may establish their absence when its certified PDDL net
effect has the required exact delete.
Mixed Boolean/numeric guards are serialized only from complete action-only net
Boolean effects and constant-integer numeric deltas. A negated numeric equality
without a certified change-away branch remains observation-only. The integrated
runtime advances the deterministic finite automaton after the initial valuation
and every successful primitive PDDL action. Transition helpers complete on
source-state exit, so one atomic macro may cross several DFA edges without
replaying an obsolete transition. This exact observation boundary does not make
action-strategy synthesis complete for arbitrary
PDDL--LTLf products. The evaluated formula language is the declared
$F$, $X$, $U$, conjunction, and literal-negation fragment, not unrestricted
LTLf.

The pinned temporal matrix contains no observed failure category, so it cannot
measure rejection recall. Atomic structure is reported only for the exact
hash-locked libraries used by that clean run; their generation batch lacks an
eligible clean-revision timing record. We therefore do not infer a baseline
improvement from those descriptive values. The registered comparison separates
validated evidence adaptation, schema closure, decomposed candidate generation,
joint selection, preservation certification, controller structure, and monitor
observation boundary. Until those paired cells are populated, the corresponding
comparative claims remain intentionally blank.

\section{Conclusion}

This work closes a representation gap between generalized planning and BDI
execution. The compiler turns singleton-goal policy evidence and PDDL schemas
into one maintained, lifted AgentSpeak domain library, while a separate
\ltlf-to-DFA stage appends certified query-local controllers for supported
temporally extended goals and observes every primitive action. All
\TEGProblemCount{} released queries across \BenchmarkDomainCount{} domains pass
Jason, neutral-goal VAL, and both DFA checks under the declared Boolean and
bounded-integer fragments. The
contribution is not a new generalized planner or BDI semantics, but a
fail-closed compilation problem that makes reusable planning evidence
deployable as explicit BDI behavior. Broader provider evidence, longer schema
composition, multi-step resource discharge, and more complete temporal action
strategy synthesis remain future work.
